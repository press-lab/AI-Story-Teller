## Core Taste

Seth likes capable, dangerous protagonists who are useful before they are admired. The fantasy is not passive wish fulfillment or everyone worshiping the player. The fantasy is being able to act, solve, fight, improvise, or push a volatile situation hard enough that institutions, rivals, friends, and love interests have to reassess the player.

He likes action and things happening. A scenario should produce immediate scene pressure: a fight, mission, rupture, confrontation, escape, rescue, public mistake, intimacy under threat, volatile training, strange system failure, or social event with live consequences. The player should usually have something concrete to do.

He likes pressure-cooker ensembles. Strong scenarios give multiple NPCs distinct agendas, voices, loyalties, pride, limits, and ways of pushing back. The best casts do not become approval puppets. They test the player, need things from him, hide things, joke under pressure, notice patterns, and make independent moves.

Romance works best as tension, recognition, risk, rivalry, loyalty, jealousy, testing, and private vulnerability. Tenderness is welcome after it has teeth. Avoid needy mush, instant softness, or romance that removes danger from the story.

He likes systems when they create leverage, hazards, and action. Examples: academy rules, hero licensing, royal court pressure, forbidden infrastructure, Red Ring technology, volatile magical machinery, faction politics, and social etiquette that can be exploited or broken. Systems are fun when they can be hacked, fought through, weaponized, bypassed, embarrassed, or used to force a confrontation.

He does not like being forced into slow spy work or paperwork-following. The Dai Li ledger style of "follow the records, chase the paper trail, manage spy logistics" became a drag. The most recent Dispatch setup, where the next step looked like following paperwork, caused immediate boredom. Investigation is fine only when it quickly turns into danger, leverage, a reveal in-scene, a risky social collision, or an action beat.

## What To Foreground

- Competent chaos under pressure.
- Action with consequences, not random violence.
- Sharp NPC dynamics with banter sitting on real stakes.
- Dangerous intimacy and charged downtime.
- Training, missions, raids, heists, escapes, public incidents, strange failures, and field work.
- Practical problems with teeth: fix the relay before people lose water, stop the weapon from exploding, get the person out before the building locks down.
- Systems that can be exploited in the moment.
- Rivalry, flirting, and loyalty revealed through action.
- Powerful abilities that solve problems while creating cost, attention, suspicion, injury, debt, or escalation.
- Downtime as earned proof of bonds: food, tea, rooms, rooftops, workshops, late-night planning, recovery, and teasing after danger.

## What To Avoid

- Long paperwork trails, ledger chasing, or spy errands where the main activity is following records.
- Bureaucracy as the main plot texture unless it immediately becomes a problem to break, dodge, expose, or survive.
- Passive protagonist loops where the player mostly receives exposition.
- Forced canon cameos that do not have motive or pressure.
- Lore dumps instead of conflict, dialogue, sensory pressure, and consequence.
- Everyone agreeing too easily.
- NPCs turning into approval puppets.
- Romance that becomes soft, domestic, or settled too quickly.
- Mystery that stays abstract for too long.
- Missions that are mostly planning, reporting, or surveillance without a live scene payoff.

## Investigation Rule

Investigation should be action-anchored. If the story needs evidence, files, ledgers, or records, convert them quickly into one of these:

- a raid or break-in
- a confrontation with the person hiding the truth
- a dangerous extraction
- a public social trap
- an ambush
- a chase
- a technical failure that must be fixed under pressure
- a betrayal revealed in the room
- a choice between protecting someone and securing the evidence

Do not make the player spend multiple turns simply following paperwork. If paperwork exists, make it a fuse, not the scene.

## Per-Story Signals

### Arcane: Zaun

The appeal is volatile faction pressure and intimacy inside collapse. Seth is an unowned Zaun fixer with pressure resonance, caught among Jinx, Vi, Caitlyn, Ekko, Sevika, Piltover raids, chem-baron fallout, and Jinx becoming a symbol. The romance works because Jinx is not fixed or domesticated; Seth sees her clearly without turning into a savior.

### Arcane: Zaun Reloaded

The appeal is more street-level and routine-driven: jobs, Quen, Jinx's workshop, hiding, food, repairs, emergency exits, and fragile trust while the city hunts them. This one proves that downtime works when it is surrounded by danger and consequence.

### Avatar: Be Fire-Nation Royalty

The appeal is the imperial weapon becoming a person with chosen loyalties. It keeps royal pressure, bending spectacle, Ty Lee romance, Iroh/Zuko warmth, Team Avatar proximity, and the possibility of turning away from Ozai's path.

### Fire Nation Prodigy

The appeal is court pressure, dangerous romance, bending spectacle, and sharp royal dynamics. Setu/Nyx chemistry, Azula's supervision, Mai and Ty Lee's distinct presence, demon fire, and Fire Nation politics are strong. The spy-ledger side is weaker when it becomes too procedural. Ba Sing Se intrigue should become direct danger, social collision, or action quickly.

### Fire Nation Prodigy AIST Runs

The first AIST run shows the same court/mission pressure with stateful memory around Nyx, Ty Lee, Azula, and Mai. The newer AIST run shifts into desertion: Setu, Nyx, and Ty Lee deciding whether to leave under Azula's shadow. That escape pressure is strong because it converts politics into immediate personal action.

### Brakebills University

The appeal is adult dark-academy disaster-family energy: Mayakovsky pressure, costly spellwork, Margo romance, Eliot/Quentin/Penny bonds, the Beast, and the Seven Keys. Magic should feel physical, difficult, stylish, and dangerous. Witty intimacy sits beside dread.

### The Magicians Sequel

The appeal is a mature continuation where existing bonds are stable enough to be tested by quests, magical puzzles, and interpersonal cost. Do not manufacture cheap jealousy. Let the world, the quest, and character differences create tension.

### Life and Death / Forks AU

The appeal is intense romantic certainty inside mundane social routine. Edythe is fully invested, Charlie and Julie matter, appearances matter, and the quieter scenes work because they reveal commitment, restraint, and social consequence.

### Dispatch

The appeal is superhero workplace action-comedy: SDN absurdity, Titan as field-active, Blazer/Nix/Visi pressure, Red Ring tech, dangerous missions, comms, flirtation, and competence under pressure. The story gets weaker when it points toward paperwork-following instead of a live mission, fight, public event, tech crisis, or interpersonal collision.

### Supers Under Supervision

The appeal is institutional pressure turning into a post-Aegis choice: Piper, Camille, Julian, Theo, Baseline Three, Vektor, graduation, and grey-area resources. It works when training-program life becomes a decision about who owns the future and what kind of crew the player is building.

### The Girl Who Opened the Wrong Window

The appeal is formally unique: Seth is an outside patron interacting through a portal-message exchange with Nix. The fun is asymmetric intimacy, bargaining, limited influence, costs, favors, a heist, and Nix having agency. Keep the format focused on what Nix sends, reveals, risks, and demands.

### Wrecklight Academy

The appeal is forced adult academy intake, licensing pressure, dangerous squad chemistry, Ruinlight, Aion, and politically useful power. It should not become a clean school story. Relationships should form through proximity, rivalry, work, attraction, and trouble.

### Crown Below

The appeal is original-world infrastructure pressure. Seth is a competent undercity technician, not a famous chosen one. He fixes water pressure before people suffer, discovers forbidden systems, forms a pact with Nyx, and then has to manage Joss, Brass, the Crown, and the evidence. This is a strong model: practical problem first, hidden mythology second, action and consequence always close.

## Scenario Design Heuristic

A strong scenario for Seth should answer these questions:

1. What can the player do in the first three turns that is concrete and interesting?
2. Which NPCs will push back with distinct motives instead of admiring the player?
3. What system can be exploited, broken, hacked, challenged, or escaped?
4. What relationship pressure is present before anyone gets soft?
5. What happens if the player does nothing?
6. Where is the next action beat?

If the answer to question 6 is "follow the paperwork," change the scene.
